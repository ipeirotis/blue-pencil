# Altitude

Load this when a passage carries detail its section does not want: an abstract or introduction clogged with statistical machinery, a topic sentence that opens on a caveat, a summary paragraph stacking secondary numbers, or a high-level claim trailing its own confidence interval. The Subtraction reference (`references/subtraction.md`) decides whether a unit earns its place at all; this file decides which section a unit that does earn its place belongs in. The two questions are independent: a caveat can be load-bearing, so the keep-test keeps it, and still sit three sections too high, so the altitude test moves it.

## The altitude of a passage

Every passage sits at an altitude, and detail has a home. High-altitude text (the abstract, section openers, topic sentences, the contribution paragraph) states findings and claims. Low-altitude text (Methods, Results, the body of a section) carries the machinery that supports each finding: the model class, the estimator, specification sweeps, confidence intervals, design caveats, single-source limitations. Obtuse prose is almost always detail that floated up out of its home section. The reader meets the inferential apparatus before they have any reason to want it, and the claim it was built to support is buried inside it.

The fix is not to compress the detail. It is to move it down to the section that wants it. A clause pitched three sections below its passage is a relocation, not a word cut: send it home and note the move in `Change rationale`. Two gates bind the move. Scope: relocate only when the home section is inside the unit you were asked to edit; when the home is out of scope or the manuscript has no home for the clause, raise the move in `Author questions` rather than performing an out-of-scope edit or cutting the clause, and this holds for ordinary machinery and secondary statistics, not only for a caveat. Stage: relocation is structural, so perform it only at first draft or on a whole-section pass; at final polish, and outside the flagged paragraphs of a response-to-reviewers pass, flag the altitude issue in `Diagnosis` or `Author questions` and move nothing. Even inside a response-to-reviewers pass, a relocation edits two paragraphs, so perform it only when both the clause's source paragraph and its destination paragraph are flagged; when the destination paragraph is unflagged, moving the clause would write into text the reviewers did not flag, so route the move to `Author questions` instead. Moving a numerical or statistical clause still trips the numerical-claim constraint, so flag it there as well.

## Three directives

**Report the finding, not the machinery.** Above the Results section, a result is direction plus magnitude plus one significance marker. The model class and the level at which inference runs belong in the manuscript's Methods or statistical-analysis section, and the confidence interval rides down to Results; send each clause to the section the manuscript actually uses for it rather than defaulting all of the apparatus to Results. One exception: when `target_venue` requires a structured abstract, its own Methods subsection (above Results but inside the abstract) is the home for study design, model class, and inference level, so keep those details under that heading rather than moving them into the manuscript body. One part of the apparatus is not machinery: a robustness or specification sweep that changes the conclusion. When an effect is clean on the primary specification but borderline on another (significant on one scale, p = 0.04 to 0.07 on the raw scale), that fragility is part of the finding, so keep a marker of it at altitude or flag it in `Author questions`; never simplify a borderline result into a clean one. A sweep rides down only when it confirms the headline across specifications.

Too high (abstract): "a mixed-effects model on log completion time, with inference at the participant level where treatment was assigned, estimates a 31% reduction per query (p = 0.03, 95% CI 4% to 51%); the raw-scale estimate of 212 s is borderline across inferential specifications (p = 0.04 to 0.07)."

At altitude: "participants took an estimated 31% less time per query (p = 0.03), though the effect is borderline on the raw scale." The model class and inference level are the Methods section's job and the interval is the Results section's job, but the raw-scale fragility is not: it changes how robust the finding is, so a marker of it stays or goes to `Author questions`, never dropped to make the result read clean. Simplify the wording, never the estimand: a 31% cut in completion time is not a 31% gain in speed (the throughput change is larger), and dropping the "per query" unit would blur a per-query effect into total session time, so keep both the quantity and its unit as the source measured them. Keep the evidential status too: a modeled or adjusted value is an estimate, so a light marker ("estimated", "we estimate") stays even though the model itself goes down. Recasting a number, stripping its unit, or dropping its estimate status changes a numerical claim and trips the numerical-claim constraint.

**Send each caveat to its home section.** A design qualification belongs in Methods; a single-annotator or overall sample-size limitation belongs in Limitations. Never voice such a caveat at the moment you first state the claim it qualifies: the reader has not yet grasped the claim, so the hedge lands as noise, and a preemptive limitation reads as apology where a well-placed one would read as competence. One kind of caveat is the exception, because it calibrates the headline claim itself rather than the study in general: a power or sample-size qualifier on a null or near-null finding ("no detectable difference at this sample size") is scope by the reading-out test in `references/subtraction.md`, since relocating it lets "no detectable effect" read as "no effect". Keep that one with its claim at altitude, or route the move to `Author questions`; never file it under Limitations. Relocation is not deletion in any case: a caveat is content (constraint 6). Move it only when its home section already carries it, or is in scope for you to place it there. When you cannot confirm that (the destination is outside the requested unit, or the manuscript states the caveat nowhere else), the abstract's caveat is the only visible scope signal, so raise the move in `Author questions` rather than cutting the signal and letting the claim broaden.

Too high (abstract): "each transcript coded by a single annotator"; "each task carried a hint that disambiguated its phrasing for both arms, so our results describe performance under disambiguated task framing."

At altitude: move the disambiguation design to Methods and the single-coder limitation to Limitations, once you confirm each is stated (or can be stated) there. If neither is, and the abstract is the only place the scope appears, keep it or flag the relocation in `Author questions`; the abstract's external-validity claims must not widen silently.

**Keep one number per claim; send the rest down.** A confidence interval, a range, or a secondary p-value stacked onto a single headline figure is usually a Results move: state the point estimate and one marker, and let the precision ride down into the body. Two things keep the interval at altitude, so check them first: a venue whose abstract format requires it (some structured and clinical abstracts do, per `target_venue`), and a result whose point is the precision itself, where the interval width is the finding. Absent both, the interval goes down. A range gets the keep-test too: when the spread itself is the finding (heterogeneity across subgroups, sites, or tasks, an effect that runs from beneficial to harmful), it is load-bearing and stays; a range that is only the interval around a headline average rides down.

A derived or combined metric is different, and the keep-test decides it before the altitude test moves it: ask whether the number changes the conclusion the reader draws at this altitude. Correct-answers-per-hour is derived from the two headline findings, but in a speed-versus-accuracy study it can be the combined endpoint that answers the paper's question (did the speed gain produce more correct work?); when it is, it is load-bearing and stays, derived or not. Send it down only when the headline findings already carry that conclusion on their own. A baseline or control rate behind a relative effect gets the same keep-test: "mortality fell 50%" and "mortality fell 50%, from 2% to 1%" carry the same relative number but a very different sense of the stakes, so when the denominator changes how the headline figure reads, it is load-bearing and stays with the claim rather than riding down.

Too high (abstract): "an 18-point deficit (p = 0.08) whose 95% confidence interval runs from a 39-point deficit to rough parity."

At altitude: "an 18-point deficit (p = 0.08)." The interval is a second statistic on the same claim and belongs in Results; the point estimate and one marker carry the finding at abstract altitude.

## The altitude test

For any sentence that reads dense, ask: what is this sentence's altitude, and does every clause in it belong at that altitude? A clause pitched below the passage is the edit, and the edit is to move it, not to compress it.

The altitude test composes with two neighbours. The keep-test (`references/subtraction.md`) asks whether a clause survives at all; the altitude test asks where the survivor belongs. And a finding stated once does not get clearer by being restated in fresh words: the restatement, including the "here is what it means" echo that adds no new claim, is a cut under the say-it-once rule in `references/subtraction.md`, not a relocation.

## The load-bearing sentence is the plainest

The sentence carrying a passage's main point should be the easiest sentence in it to parse, not the hardest. When the takeaway is the most nested, most clause-laden sentence in the paragraph, the reader works hardest exactly where they can least afford to. Keep the closing claim of an abstract or section short, with a concrete subject, an active verb, and no stacked subordinate clauses; push the qualifications into a following sentence. The sentence-level pattern, with a before-and-after, is "The buried thesis" in `references/sentence-patterns.md`.
